# Day 08 - Phishing and Social Engineering

## What are Phishing and Social Engineering?

Phishing is a cyberattack where attackers trick people into revealing sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, credit card details, or bank account information through fake emails, websites, or messages.

Social Engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or revealing confidential information. Instead of attacking computers directly, attackers target human behavior.

---

## Why are Phishing and Social Engineering Dangerous?

They can:

- Steal usernames and passwords.
- Gain unauthorized access to accounts.
- Steal money or financial information.
- Spread malware.
- Cause identity theft.
- Compromise personal and organizational data.

---

## Types of Phishing

### 1. Email Phishing

Attackers send fake emails pretending to be trusted organizations.

**Example:**
An email claims to be from your bank asking you to verify your account by clicking a link.

---

### 2. Spear Phishing

A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization.

**Example:**
An employee receives an email that appears to come from their manager requesting sensitive company information.

---

### 3. Smishing

Phishing conducted through SMS or text messages.

**Example:**
A text message says your bank account has been locked and asks you to click a link.

---

### 4. Vishing

Phishing conducted through phone calls.

**Example:**
A caller pretends to be from your bank and asks for your PIN or OTP.

---

## Common Social Engineering Techniques

### Pretexting

The attacker creates a fake identity or story to gain trust.

### Baiting

The attacker offers something attractive, such as free software or a free USB drive, to trick victims.

### Tailgating

An unauthorized person follows an authorized employee into a secure building.

### Impersonation

The attacker pretends to be a trusted person, such as a manager, IT technician, or police officer.

---

## Warning Signs of Phishing

- Unexpected emails requesting personal information.
- Poor grammar and spelling mistakes.
- Urgent messages demanding immediate action.
- Suspicious links or attachments.
- Unknown sender's email address.

---

## How to Protect Yourself

- Never click suspicious links.
- Verify the sender before responding.
- Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
- Keep your software updated.
- Avoid downloading unknown attachments.
- Always check website URLs before entering sensitive information.

---

## Real-Life Example

You receive an email claiming to be from your bank saying your account will be suspended unless you log in immediately. The email contains a fake website that looks identical to your bank's login page. When you enter your username and password, the attacker steals your credentials.

---

## Summary

- Phishing attacks trick users into revealing sensitive information.
- Social engineering targets human behavior instead of technology.
- Awareness and careful verification are the best defenses.
- Multi-Factor Authentication adds an extra layer of protection.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Think before you click.
- Verify emails, messages, and phone calls.
- Never share passwords or OTPs.
- Cybersecurity is about protecting both technology and people.
